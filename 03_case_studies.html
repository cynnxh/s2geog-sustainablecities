Cape Town Water Crisis

South Africa · 2015–2018 drought
Tap to reveal
  • Cape Town suffered a severe drought between 2015 and 2018.
  • In July 2018, taps were turned off for homes and businesses, and water had to be rationed as reservoir levels were dangerously low.
  • The government said it would cut off water supply completely ("Day Zero") if reservoir levels hit 13.5%.
  • The crisis was worsened by a growing urban population and poor water consumption habits.
  • The worst case was averted when it rained — by September 2018, reservoir levels rose to almost 70% and most restrictions were lifted.
🔵 Model Answer — Why did the crisis happen?The Cape Town water crisis occurred due to a severe drought combined with a rapidly growing urban population that increased water demand, along with poor water consumption habits among residents, which together caused reservoir levels to fall to dangerously low levels.

Kranji Reservoir

Singapore · Managing water pollution
Tap to reveal
  • Kranji Reservoir is located near Singapore's main agricultural area.
  • Problem: rainwater mixes with excess fertiliser from farms, and this polluted water flows into the reservoir, harming water quality.
  • Solution: drains were constructed around the farms so polluted surface run-off is diverted to treatment ponds instead of straight into the reservoir.
  • Plants such as pandan and cattails are grown on floating wetlands at the edge of the treatment ponds to help purify the water naturally.
  • This is hoped to improve water quality in Kranji Reservoir over time.
🔵 Model Answer — How is water pollution managed at Kranji Reservoir?Cut-off drains were built around the farms to channel polluted run-off (containing excess fertiliser) into treatment ponds instead of the reservoir. Floating wetlands planted with pandan and cattails at the ponds help to naturally filter and purify the water before it enters the reservoir, improving overall water quality.

China's Clean Air Laws

Beijing, Tianjin & Hebei · Managing air pollution
Tap to reveal
  • Cities in China suffered serious air pollution from industrial activities and vehicular emissions.
  • In 2013, China implemented the Air Pollution Prevention and Control Action Plan for Beijing, Tianjin and Hebei.
  • The plan lets cities regulate the actions of citizens and industries.
  • A license plate lottery policy was introduced, limiting the number of new vehicle registrations.
  • Vehicle restriction rules were also rolled out — limiting private car use to selected days of the week, based on the last digit of the license plate.
🔵 Model Answer — How does China manage air pollution?China introduced the Air Pollution Prevention and Control Action Plan (2013) to regulate citizens and industries in Beijing, Tianjin and Hebei. This included a license plate lottery to limit new vehicle registrations, and vehicle restriction rules that limit private cars to selected days based on their license plate numbers — reducing the number of vehicles on the road and vehicular emissions.

Singapore's Housing Story

Singapore · From squatters to HDB flats
Tap to reveal
  • In the 1950s–1960s, Singapore had a housing shortage. Large numbers of people lived in squatter settlements.
  • Squatter settlements had a low standard of living: no running water/electricity, poor sanitation, overcrowding, and diseases spread easily.
  • These settlements developed due to rapid population growth — caused by both natural increase and migration.
  • The Singapore Improvement Trust, later succeeded by the Housing & Development Board (HDB), solved the problem by building affordable public housing and resettling squatters.
  • By 2020, over 80% of Singapore's population lived in HDB-built homes, often surrounded by good amenities — schools, shopping malls, recreational parks and healthcare providers.
🔵 Model Answer — How did Singapore solve its housing problem?Singapore's housing shortage in the 1950s–60s led to overcrowded, unsanitary squatter settlements caused by rapid population growth. The Singapore Improvement Trust, later replaced by the Housing & Development Board (HDB), addressed this by building affordable public housing and resettling squatters. By 2020, over 80% of Singaporeans lived in HDB flats with good access to amenities such as schools, malls and healthcare, greatly improving quality of life.
